Business Plans AO1

To set out a business's objectives, strategy, and financial projections — both to guide the founders and to convince lenders/investors the business is viable.

A typical business plan includes: the business idea, objectives, market research findings, marketing plan, operations plan, financial forecasts (sales, costs, cash flow), and funding required.

Writing a business plan
AdvantagesDrawbacks
Forces the entrepreneur to think through risks and viability before committing moneyTakes significant time to research and write properly
Required by most banks/investors before they will lendForecasts are often inaccurate, especially for a brand-new business with no track record
Gives a benchmark to measure actual performance againstA plan can quickly become outdated if market conditions change

Forms of Business & Liability AO1

FormLiabilityKey feature
Sole traderUnlimitedOne owner, full control, full personal risk
PartnershipUnlimited (usually)2+ owners sharing control, risk, and profit
Private limited company (Ltd)LimitedShares not sold to the public; owners' personal assets protected
Public limited company (Plc)LimitedShares sold publicly on a stock exchange; can raise large amounts of capital

Limited liability means an owner can only lose what they invested in the business — personal assets (house, savings) are protected if the business fails. Unlimited liability means personal assets are at risk if the business cannot pay its debts.

Operating with limited liability (Ltd/Plc)
AdvantagesDrawbacks
Owners' personal assets are protected from business debtsMore legal/administrative requirements (e.g. filing accounts)
Easier to raise finance by selling sharesFinancial information becomes publicly available
Can encourage more risk-taking/investment by ownersPlcs risk takeover if shares are bought up by another company

Internal Finance AO1

To fund the business using resources it already controls, avoiding the cost and obligations of borrowing.

SourceDescription
Personal savingsOwner's own money invested into the business
Retained profitProfit kept in the business rather than paid out, reinvested
Sale of assetsSelling unused business assets (e.g. equipment, property) for cash
Using internal finance
AdvantagesDrawbacks
No interest payments or debt obligationsLimited by how much the business/owner actually has available
No loss of control (no new shareholders or lender conditions)Using savings/retained profit reduces the cash cushion available for emergencies

External Finance AO1

SourceDescription
Bank loanBorrowed sum repaid with interest over an agreed period
OverdraftShort-term flexible borrowing on a bank account up to an agreed limit
Share capitalMoney raised by selling shares (ownership) in the business
Venture capitalInvestment from a specialist investor, usually in exchange for equity
CrowdfundingRaising small amounts from a large number of people, often online
Trade creditBuying supplies now and paying the supplier later (e.g. in 30 days)
Using a bank loan
AdvantagesDrawbacks
Provides a lump sum upfront for larger investmentsMust be repaid with interest regardless of how the business performs
Owner retains full ownership (unlike share capital)May require collateral/security, risking assets if repayments are missed
Using share capital
AdvantagesDrawbacks
No requirement to repay or pay interestDilutes ownership and control (new shareholders get a say)
Can raise very large sums (especially for a Plc)Profits must be shared via dividends

Retained profitProfit kept in the business rather than paid out, used for reinvestment
