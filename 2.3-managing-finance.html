Profit AO1

To measure how much money a business has earned after all costs, the core indicator of financial success used by owners, investors and lenders.

TermFormula
Gross profitRevenue − Cost of sales
Operating profitGross profit − Operating expenses
Net profitOperating profit − Interest & Tax

These appear on the Statement of Comprehensive Income (income statement), which shows performance over a period (e.g. a year).

Liquidity AO1

To assess whether a business has enough short-term assets to pay its short-term debts — a profitable business can still fail if it runs out of liquid cash.

Liquidity is assessed using the Statement of Financial Position (balance sheet), which shows assets, liabilities and equity at a point in time.

RatioFormulaWhat it shows
Current ratioCurrent assets ÷ Current liabilitiesAbility to pay short-term debts (ideal ~1.5–2)
Acid test ratio(Current assets − Inventory) ÷ Current liabilitiesStricter liquidity test, excluding stock

Ratio Analysis AO2

To assess different aspects of a business's financial performance by comparing figures from its financial statements over time or against competitors.

RatioFormulaWhat it shows
Gross profit margin(Gross profit ÷ Revenue) × 100Profitability of core trading activity
Net profit margin(Net profit ÷ Revenue) × 100Overall profitability after all costs
ROCE(Operating profit ÷ Capital employed) × 100How efficiently capital generates profit
Gearing ratio(Non-current liabilities ÷ Capital employed) × 100Proportion of finance from debt vs equity
Using ratio analysis
AdvantagesDrawbacks
Allows comparison over time and against competitors/industryRatios alone don't explain the underlying causes
Highlights potential problems early (e.g. worsening liquidity)Based on historic data, which may not reflect current conditions
Useful for investors/lenders assessing riskDifferent accounting policies between businesses can distort comparisons

Business Failure AO1

To understand the causes of business failure, so risks can be identified and managed in advance.

Internal causesExternal causes
Poor cash flow managementEconomic downturn/recession
Weak financial control/planningNew competitor entry
Poor management decisionsChanges in regulation/law
