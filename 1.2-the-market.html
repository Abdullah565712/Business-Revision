Demand AO1

To understand what determines how much of a product customers are willing and able to buy.

Demand = the quantity of a good/service consumers are willing and able to buy at a given price, over a given time period.

  • Price of the product
  • Income levels
  • Price of substitutes/complements
  • Tastes and fashion
  • Advertising
  • Population/demographics
  • Interest rates

A change in price causes a movement along the demand curve. A change in any other factor shifts the whole curve.

Supply AO1

To understand what determines how much businesses are willing and able to produce/sell.

Supply = the quantity of a good/service producers are willing and able to sell at a given price, over a given time period.

  • Cost of production
  • Technology/productivity
  • Number of producers
  • Government regulation/taxes/subsidies
  • Weather (agricultural)

Demand & Supply Interaction AO2

To explain how market price and quantity are determined, and what happens when conditions change.

  • Equilibrium price: the price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
  • Excess demand: below equilibrium, demand exceeds supply, pushing price up.
  • Excess supply: above equilibrium, supply exceeds demand, pushing price down.

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) AO1

To measure how sensitive customer demand is to price changes, helping businesses set pricing strategy.

PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price

  • Price elastic (PED > 1): demand very responsive to price (e.g. luxury goods, many substitutes).
  • Price inelastic (PED < 1): demand not very responsive (e.g. necessities, few substitutes).
Using PED in pricing decisions
AdvantagesDrawbacks
Helps predict revenue effect of a price changePED can change over time, making past data unreliable
Supports more profitable pricing strategyDifficult to calculate accurately in practice
Identifies products that can bear price increasesIgnores other demand factors (e.g. competitor actions)

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED) AO1

To measure how sensitive demand is to changes in consumer income.

YED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in income

  • Positive YED: normal good. YED>1 = luxury/income elastic; 0<YED<1 = necessity/income inelastic.
  • Negative YED: inferior good (demand falls as income rises, e.g. value brands).
Using YED
AdvantagesDrawbacks
Helps anticipate impact of economic growth/recessionConsumer income data/behaviour can be volatile
Supports product range decisions across economic conditionsDoesn't account for changing tastes independent of income
Identifies if a product is luxury, necessity, or inferiorHistorical YED may not hold true in future conditions
